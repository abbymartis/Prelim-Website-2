Technology Choices

​​Social media has revolutionized human interaction​, creating boundless opportunities for connectivity, ​self-expression, and global communication. However​, alongside these benefits lies growing risks associated with identity theft and phishing scams. As ​platforms such as Facebook, Instagram​, and Twitter become embedded in everyday life, users are increasingly vulnerable to cyber threats. Among them, phishing stands out as one of the most pervasive and deceptive tactics. Yet, users are not powerless. A range of informed choices can significantly reduce exposure and empower individuals to protect themselves in the digital age.

Phishing, a deceptive ​technique used to trick individuals into revealing​ personal ​information such as passwords or credit card numbers​, has evolved rapidly on social networking sites (SNSs). Silic and Back (2016) emphasize that phishing on social media is uniquely dangerous because users often lower their guard in these informal, trust-driven environments. Unlike traditional phishing, which may rely on suspicious-looking emails, social media phishing usually appears to come from friends, coworkers, or familiar brands, making it far more effective and harder to detect.

One of the most critical choices available to users is deciding how much personal information they share. Alharbi et al. (2021) highlight that oversharing on SNSs creates a goldmine for identity thieves, who use this information to impersonate users or craft targeted phishing campaigns. Choosing to restrict profile visibility, limit public posts, and regularly audit what information is available online are powerful steps that reduce vulnerability to these threats.

Users also have the choice to engage in cyber awareness education. According to a recent study published on DOAJ, users who completed anti-phishing training reported greater confidence in recognizing phishing attempts and navigating online threats. Despite this, many users cite barriers such as lack of time or interest. However, the same study found that short, targeted education modules—like digital nudges or micro-learning tools—can effectively raise awareness without overwhelming users. Choosing to participate in these programs, even briefly, can significantly improve personal cybersecurity.

Another vital choice is how users respond to suspicious activity. Thomas Nagunwa (2014) outlines how phishing vectors often exploit trust and time pressure to prompt rash decisions. Users can choose to pause, verify messages through other channels, and report suspicious links or profiles directly to the platform. Additionally, enabling two-factor authentication (2FA) is an increasingly accessible option that provides an extra layer of defense, even if login credentials are compromised.

Beyond individual action, there is a growing need for collective responsibility. Alharbi et al. (2021) argue that platform providers also play a role in protecting users through better detection tools and real-time alerts. Still, these measures can only do so much without active user engagement. Ultimately, each user’s choices contribute to a safer digital ecosystem. In conclusion, while phishing and identity theft on social media are growing threats, individuals are not helpless. Through conscious decisions—managing data privacy, participating in awareness training, verifying messages, and using enhanced security settings—users can defend themselves against the evolving landscape of social media-based phishing. The choices are clear, and making them is a crucial step toward digital safety.
